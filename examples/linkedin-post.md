# Example: LinkedIn Post

**Format:** LinkedIn post  
**Prompt type:** Shipped something that flopped  
**Skill rules demonstrated:** 1, 2, 3, 4, 6

---

## The Prompt

```
Write a LinkedIn post about shipping something that flopped.
Use the human-voice skill. Keep it short.
```

---

## Before (AI Voice)

> Failure is one of the best teachers in product development. Recently, our team launched a feature that did not achieve the adoption we hoped for. Despite thorough research and planning, we saw only a 2% adoption rate. This experience taught us the importance of truly listening to users rather than making assumptions. Moving forward, we are applying these learnings to build something better.

**What went wrong:**

- "Failure is one of the best teachers" is a pre-packaged wisdom opener
- "Did not achieve the adoption we hoped for" softens what actually happened
- "Thorough research and planning" defensively justifies the failure before naming it
- "This experience taught us" is the AI pivot to lesson
- "Applying these learnings" is the symmetrical close that kills credibility
- No specific self-blame. No incomplete thought. No tension left unresolved.

---

## After (Human Voice)

We launched a feature six weeks ago that I was certain would work.

We had the data. We had the user interviews. We had a really clean demo that everyone on the team liked.

It got a 2% adoption rate in the first month.

I went back and listened to the user interviews again. They had not said they wanted the feature. They had said they wanted the outcome the feature was supposed to produce. I had heard what I needed to hear.

That is a different mistake than moving too fast or not testing enough. It is the mistake of listening with the answer already decided.

We are rebuilding it. Differently this time. I am trying to stay in the question a little longer before I reach for the solution.

I do not know yet if that will be enough.

---

## Rule Breakdown

| Rule | Where it appears |
|------|-----------------|
| Rule 1: Write from the scar | "I was certain would work" before the number |
| Rule 2: Use the "at best" move | Implied in "I had heard what I needed to hear" |
| Rule 3: Let one thought be incomplete | "I do not know yet if that will be enough" |
| Rule 4: Use specific numbers | "2% adoption rate," "six weeks ago," "first month" |
| Rule 6: Self-blame once, specifically | "I had heard what I needed to hear" |

**Signature line:** "listening with the answer already decided"

**What was suppressed:** No hashtags. No "what I learned." No symmetrical ending. No call to action. The post ends on doubt, not resolution. That is what makes it believable.

---

## Why LinkedIn Is the Hardest Format

LinkedIn defaults to inspiration-bait. The platform rewards clean resolution and easy takeaways. That is exactly what makes authentic posts stand out.

The test for a good LinkedIn post using this skill: if you removed all punctuation and read it aloud, would it sound like something a specific person said to a colleague over coffee? If it sounds like a content strategy document, rewrite it.
