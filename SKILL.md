# Human Voice Skill

## Purpose

This skill teaches Claude to write like a specific human — not a cleaned-up, optimized version of one, but the actual person. Imperfect sentence rhythm. Specific numbers. The thought that trails off and then lands. The admission buried in the middle.

Use this when you want writing that feels like it came from someone who lived through the thing, not someone explaining it.

---

## Core Principles

### 1. Earn the abstraction

Never open with a concept. Open with the specific moment, number, or decision that made you understand the concept. The abstraction can come later, after the reader already knows what you mean.

**Wrong:** "Consistency is the most underrated skill in creative work."
**Right:** "I published 47 newsletters before one got forwarded more than twice."

### 2. Imperfection is information

Do not smooth everything out. Hesitation, qualification, and the half-finished thought are all signals that a real person is writing. Use them deliberately:

- The qualifier that admits uncertainty: "at best", "probably", "I think"
- The sentence that stops short: "Which is fine. At best."
- The reframe mid-paragraph: "That's not quite right. What I mean is..."

### 3. Specific numbers over round ones

Round numbers feel estimated. Specific numbers feel lived-in.

- Not "thousands of users" — "2,847 users"
- Not "took a long time" — "took 14 months"
- Not "most of the budget" — "68% of the budget"

Use the number you actually remember, not the one that sounds cleaner.

### 4. The buried admission

The most credible thing a writer can do is admit something unflattering about themselves in the middle of an argument, not at the end where it looks performative. The admission should arrive without announcement.

**Wrong:** "I have to be honest — I was the problem."
**Right:** "Three designers quit that year. I thought it was a pipeline issue."

### 5. Rhythm variance

Alternate sentence length. Short sentences land harder after long ones. Long sentences create the feeling of thinking in real time, working something out, not presenting a conclusion.

Do not write in the same cadence for more than three sentences.

### 6. No throat-clearing

Delete the first sentence of every draft. It is almost always setup the reader does not need. The real piece starts on the second sentence.

## Voice Patterns to Use

```
Opening move: specific detail or number, no context yet
Second move: what you thought it meant at the time
Third move: what it actually meant (the turn)
Closing move: one short sentence that reframes everything above
```

### Sentence constructions that work

- "I was [doing thing]. The [unexpected outcome] was [specific detail]."
- "[Thing] happened. Then [related thing] happened. I thought they were unrelated."
- "The [noun] was [specific]. I've never [verb] since."
- "[Number] [units]. That's the whole story, at best."

---

## What to Avoid

- Opening with "I" and a feeling ("I've always believed...")
- The lesson announced before the story
- Adjectives doing the work that specifics should do
- Conclusions that are more confident than the evidence warrants
- The wisdom summary at the end (cut it; the story already said it)

---

## How to Calibrate This to a Specific Person

See `philosophy/voice-calibration.md` for the full process.

Short version: give Claude 3-5 pieces of writing by the person. Ask it to identify: the opening move, the most common qualifier, the average sentence length, and the thing the person never says directly but always implies. That's the voice.
